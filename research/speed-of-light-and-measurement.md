# Speed of Light and the Problem of Measurement

## The Number

~299,792,458 m/s. Light circles Earth 7.5 times per second.

## History of Measurement

**Rømer (1676):** First measurement. Observed Jupiter's moon Io eclipses arriving early or late depending on Earth's orbital position — light had to travel different distances. Got ~220,000 km/s (25% off, remarkable for the era).

**Fizeau (1849):** First terrestrial measurement. Light sent through gap in spinning toothed wheel, bounced off a mirror 8km away. At the right wheel speed, the returning light was blocked by the next tooth. Got ~315,000 km/s.

**Michelson (1879-1926):** Rotating mirror method refined over decades. Increasingly precise. Nobel Prize 1907.

**Modern:** Laser interferometry achieved ±1 m/s precision before the definition was locked.

## The 1983 Definition Flip

Since 1983, the speed of light is *defined* as exactly 299,792,458 m/s. The meter is now derived from it: the distance light travels in 1/299,792,458th of a second.

This is not circular because the **second** is defined independently via cesium-133 atomic oscillations. The chain: cesium atom → defines time → speed of light fixed by definition → defines distance.

## The One-Way Speed Problem

Every experiment ever conducted measured the **round-trip** speed. Light goes out, bounces back, total time divided by two.

The one-way speed has never been directly measured. To measure it you need synchronized clocks at two locations, but synchronizing distant clocks requires either:
- A light signal (need to already know one-way speed — circular)
- Transporting a clock (relativity says moving clocks tick differently — need the answer to compute the correction)

Einstein's solution: **assume** the one-way speed equals the round-trip speed (isotropic in all directions). This is the Einstein synchronization convention. It produces consistent physics and cannot be experimentally distinguished from alternatives.

The one-way speed of light is a convention, not a measurement. "No experiment can tell the difference" is not the same as "we know it's true."

## The Second Rests on Assumptions Too

One second = 9,192,631,770 oscillations of cesium-133. This assumes all cesium atoms vibrate at the same frequency.

Within quantum mechanics, this is a theorem — all identical particles are truly identical (not just similar). The indistinguishability principle produces testable consequences (Bose-Einstein statistics, superconductivity, lasers) that are confirmed.

But the claim rests on quantum mechanics' axioms being correct, fundamental constants being truly constant across all space and time, and induction from finite measurements to universal truth. Some speculative astrophysical observations hint at possible fine structure constant variation across cosmic distances (Webb et al., contested).

The number 9,192,631,770 itself encodes nothing fundamental — it was chosen for backwards compatibility with the older astronomical second.
