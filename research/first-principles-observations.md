# Things Humans / Living Species Observe as First Principles

A raw catalog of the most basic facts a living thing is subject to or observes,
before any theory is built on top. The point is to collect the ground-level
material so we can later sort which facts Abby should be *given* (as sensation or
built-in condition) and which it should *derive* on its own. Long is fine; this is
meant to be exhaustive, not tidy.

## Big caveat (read first)

**Every fact below is written in the convention format humans invented** — in
English words, sometimes with named objects ("sun," "water," "blue"). That is a
convention layer we are otherwise trying to keep away from Abby. For *this*
cataloging job it is fine, because the words are just handles. What matters is the
**raw body under the word-clothing**: the sensation or regularity itself, which
would be the same for a creature with no language. When we later feed these to
Abby, we feed the *body* (the felt condition or the sensed regularity), never the
words. See `representation-and-conventions.md` and
`foundations-buried-by-working-math.md` for why the clothing is agreed but the
body is the world being consistent.

So read each line as: *"here is a word-handle for a raw thing Abby would feel or
notice directly."*

## The test for "is it foundational?"

A fact earns a place here only if it passes one of three gates:

1. **Lived condition** — a state the creature is *subject to* and feels directly
   from the inside (hunger, pain). No concept needed to have it; you just have it.
2. **Raw sensory given** — something the senses deliver before any naming (a patch
   of brightness, a loud sound). The *distinction* is real even if the *label* is
   convention.
3. **Brute regularity** — a pattern you can notice by watching, with no prior
   concept required (things dropped fall; the bright thing returns).

If having the fact requires *already having a concept*, it is **derived**, and it
goes in the "excluded / contested" section, not the main list.

## A. Felt internal conditions (lived facts — felt from the inside)

These are the strongest foundations: no observation even required, the creature
simply undergoes them. This is where the "decay seed" shows up concretely.

- Hunger — a recurring internal need; must take in food to keep operating.
- Thirst — a recurring internal need; must take in liquid. (This, not "attraction
  to water," is the primitive. Seeking water is *derived* from thirst + learning.)
- Need to breathe — near-constant; distress rises fast when air is withheld.
- Tiredness / need to rest and sleep — builds with time and effort, relieved by rest.
- Pain — a sharp signal that something is damaging the body; pulls action to stop it.
- Temperature discomfort — too hot or too cold both feel bad; drive to return to comfort.
- Need to eliminate waste — builds, then relief.
- Effort / exertion cost — acting takes something out of you; hard actions tire you.
- Comfort vs discomfort in general — an ever-present felt "better/worse" gradient.
- Growth — over many cycles the body gets bigger and more capable (felt and observed).
- Aging / running down — over very many cycles the body slows and weakens (slow, one-way).
- Injury and healing — the body can be damaged; some damage repairs over time, some doesn't.
- Arousal / calm — an internal "revved up vs settled" state that shifts with events.

## B. Raw sensory givens (what the senses deliver before naming)

The labels (colors, notes, flavors) are convention; the *felt differences* are raw.

- Light vs dark — the most basic visual distinction; presence or absence of light.
- Brightness gradations — dim to intense.
- Color differences — the visual field varies in a quality we later name (hues). The
  raw fact is *these two patches look different*, not "this is blue."
- Edges / contrast — places where the visual field changes sharply.
- Motion — parts of the visual field change position over moments.
- Sound — vibrations we hear; loud vs soft; high vs low; sudden vs sustained.
- Silence — absence of sound.
- Smell — distinct qualities in the air, pleasant or repellent.
- Taste — distinct qualities in what we take in (sweet, bitter, sour, salt, savory as
  raw families; the names are convention, the distinctions are given).
- Touch — pressure, contact, texture (rough/smooth), wet/dry.
- Temperature on the skin — hot/cold sensed at the boundary.
- Body sense — we feel where our body is and how it is arranged, without looking
  (position and movement of our own limbs).
- Balance / falling — a felt sense of upright vs tipping, and the lurch of falling.
- The boundary — we feel where "I" ends and "not-I" begins; inside vs outside.

Spatial and geometric givens (well-supported as foundational: Spelke lists
*places*/space and *forms*/geometry as two separate innate core systems). These
are P0-critical because a clock is an angular, rotational, spatial object and
drawing is spatial production:

- Spatial arrangement — the visual field has a *where*, not just a *what*. Things
  sit relative to each other: above/below, beside, near/far, left/right of one
  another.
- Orientation / angle — an edge or line points some way; two edges meet at a wider
  or narrower opening. Raw fact: "this points differently than that."
- Shape / closed form — some regions of the field are bounded wholes (a round
  thing, a straight thing), distinct from their surroundings.
- Rotation — a thing can turn in place: its orientation changes over moments while
  it stays put. (This is the clock-hand primitive — angular motion.)

## C. Brute regularities of the world (patterns you can just watch)

- A bright, warm thing appears on one side, crosses, disappears into dark, and
  reliably returns — the light/dark cycle. (Your "big star" fact, stripped of the
  words "star" and "east.")
- It is warmer and brighter while that thing is up; colder and darker when it is gone.
- A dimmer light appears in the dark stretch and slowly changes shape over many cycles
  (the moon), plus many tiny fixed lights (stars).
- Released things fall — reliably in one direction (down). Never sideways, never up.
- Unsupported things drop; supported things stay.
- Things at rest stay put unless something moves them; moving things slow and stop.
- Solid things have surfaces you cannot pass through; you bump into them.
- Liquid flows downhill, pools, and takes the shape of what holds it.
- Things can be wet or dry, and wetness spreads and later disappears (drying).
- Some things are hot; heat spreads from hot to cold; hot things can hurt.
- Sounds come from events — something happens, then a sound arrives.
- Big/heavy things are harder to move than small/light ones.
- Things left alone tend to change slowly (food rots, things wear) — mostly one-way.
- Living things move on their own; most other things only move when moved.
- Weather: water sometimes falls from above; air sometimes pushes (wind).

## D. Self-and-world interaction (agency)

- I can move parts of myself at will.
- When I move, my sensations change in ways that repeat — I can *cause* changes.
- Some things in the world respond to me; some do not.
- Doing the same thing tends to give the same result (repetition is reliable).
- My actions cost effort, and effort feeds tiredness/hunger (action ties back to A).
- I can change the world, and the world changes me — the influence goes both ways.

## E. Other agents (social)

- There are other things that move on their own, like me.
- Some of them respond to what I do or the sounds I make.
- One particular presence reliably shows up and relieves my needs (the caregiver).
  This is the raw root of trust — a consistent, need-meeting source.
- Other living things age and eventually stop moving for good (death, observed in
  others before it is understood about oneself).

## F. Change, cycle, and one-wayness (the roots of time)

- Things change — this moment is not the same as the last.
- Some changes repeat (cycles: light/dark, hunger/fullness, waking/sleeping).
- Some changes are one-way and never seen to reverse (breaking, rotting, aging).
- Events have an order — a reliable before and after.
- (These are the raw material from which duration and "time" get built — never
  given as a clock. See `foundational-blocks-universe-and-abby.md`.)

## Excluded / contested — challenging the list

Per the request to keep only foundational stuff, here is what got kept *out* of the
main list and why. Developmental psychology matters here: Elizabeth Spelke's **core
knowledge** work argues infants come with a few innate "starter" systems (for
objects, agents, places/space, geometry, number, and social beings), while other
things long assumed innate turn out to be learned or to mature. So "foundational"
is genuinely contested at the edges, and we should hold these loosely.

- **Object permanence** ("a hidden thing still exists") — do NOT list as a brute
  given. Piaget treated it as *learned* over the first 1–2 years. Newer work finds a
  *kernel* may appear by 3–4 months, but whether it is innate or an early attainment
  is still openly debated. So it is a candidate *derived* fact, not a foundation. Flag
  it; do not assume it.
- **"Living vs non-living"** as a clean category — derived. What is foundational is
  the narrower observation "some things move on their own"; the full category is built
  on top.
- **Seasons / the year** — derived. Requires tracking warmth and light over very many
  light/dark cycles. It is a long-run pattern *found*, not a raw given. (This matters:
  the P0 target of "years and calendars" sits at the derived end, built from cycles in
  section C and F.)
- **Exact number / counting** — convention, NOT foundational. Important nuance: Spelke
  lists *number* as a core system, but the innate piece is an **approximate magnitude
  sense** — more vs fewer, bigger vs smaller — which is *continuous/analog*. Exact
  integers and counting are the learned convention layered on top. This supports our
  stance that Abby's native currency is continuous relations, and discreteness is
  earned later, not given.
- **Named colors, notes, flavors** — the *labels* are convention; only the felt
  *distinctions* (section B) are foundational. Kept the distinction, dropped the names.
- **"The sun," "water," "the sky"** as objects — these are already-carved concepts.
  The foundational versions are the raw regularities and sensations underneath (a
  returning bright warm thing; a drinkable liquid that relieves thirst; a bright
  overhead region that darkens).
- **Cause and effect as a principle** — borderline. The raw fact "when I do X, Y
  reliably follows" (section D) is foundational; the general *idea* of causation is
  derived from many such instances.

## Corrections to the original seed examples

- "Big star rises on one side" → keep as a light/dark cycle; drop "star" and "one
  side" (both derived/convention).
- "Attracted to transparent liquid we call water" → **corrected.** The primitive is
  **thirst** (section A). Water-seeking is derived from thirst + learning what relieves
  it. Moved accordingly.
- "Hungry feeling since birth, must eat to continue operating" → kept as-is; one of
  the strongest foundations (the decay seed, felt).
- "See colors; sky is blue, night is black" → kept the raw distinctions (light/dark,
  differing hues) in section B; dropped the labels and "sky" as convention.

## Foundational but deliberately parked (out of P0 scope)

- **Reproduction / mating drive** — genuinely first-principles: we built in
  "survive" (the decay seed), and reproduction is its evolutionary twin, the other
  half of the biological floor. But it contributes nothing to time or clocks, so it
  is **parked for P0**. Note the split: *attachment/bonding to a caregiver* (early
  "love") is NOT parked — it is the raw root of trust (section E) and we need it.
  Only the *mating/reproduction drive* is set aside. Parking it costs P0 nothing,
  because the population-level selection we lean on elsewhere ("surviving Abbys
  behave as if they have goals") does not require Abby to *feel* a drive to
  reproduce — selection can install drives without the organism wanting them. If we
  ever needed Abby to grow genuinely new goals beyond survival, this is where a
  second built-in drive would enter.
- **Curiosity / novelty-seeking** — infants orient to the new. Possibly
  foundational, but likely *emergent* from the predict-correct loop (a surprise is a
  big prediction error, which naturally pulls attention). Left off the built-in list
  on the bet that it falls out for free; revisit if it doesn't.

## P0 validation — does the catalog cover what P0 needs?

P0 = continuously learn + integrate memory + understand analog clocks + grasp
time + draw the time accurately. Mapping each demand to the catalog:

- **Concept of time** ← change, cycles, one-wayness (section F) + felt decay
  (section A). **Covered, solid.**
- **Online learning + memory** ← the predict-correct loop and memory-as-
  representation. These are *mechanism*, not observed facts, so they live in
  `experiments/experiment_1/building-blocks.md`, not this catalog. **Covered
  elsewhere.**
- **Draw the time** ← action / causing change (section D) + seeing the mark
  (sensorimotor loop, D) + spatial production (section B spatial givens).
  **Covered** after the spatial patch.
- **Read an analog clock** ← spatial + geometric perception (section B: arrangement,
  orientation/angle, shape, rotation) + cycle (F) + mapping an outside cycle onto
  inside felt duration (derived). **Was the gap; now patched.**

Two flags, not gaps:

1. **P0 straddles both engines.** Reading a *human* clock needs the human
   convention (12 hours, 60 minutes, numerals) — that is Engine 2 (taught
   convention), plus the approximate→exact number bridge. So P0 is not purely a
   grounded-perception task; it also requires the convention machinery. Do not
   under-scope it.
2. **Attention / salience** is needed (to fixate the clock) but is treated as
   *emergent* from prediction (relevance = what moves the budget), not built in.

Conclusion: with the spatial/geometric givens added, the catalog covers the
foundational perceptual and felt-condition prerequisites for P0. The remaining P0
weight is in the *derived/convention* layer (clock/calendar convention, number),
which is expected — that is Abby's job to build, not ours to list.

## How this feeds the next step

Once this catalog stabilizes, the sort we want is three-way:
1. **Give as built-in condition** (Abby is subject to it): most of section A.
2. **Give as sensation** (Abby's senses deliver it): section B, and section C arrives
   *through* B over time.
3. **Let Abby derive** (never given): sections D–F largely, plus everything in the
   excluded/contested list — time, cause, objects-as-things, number, calendars.
