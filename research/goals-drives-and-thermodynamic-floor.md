# Goals, Drives, and the Thermodynamic Floor

## Humanity Has No Goal

No agreed-upon purpose. Different frameworks, different answers:

- **Biology/evolution:** Survive and reproduce. But humans override this — choosing childlessness, creating art, self-sacrifice. Evolution gave drives, not goals.
- **Religion:** Serve God, achieve enlightenment, reach salvation. Adopted frameworks, not discovered truths. Contradictory across religions.
- **Utilitarianism:** Maximize happiness. But happiness is undefined and subjective.
- **Existentialism (Sartre):** No inherent goal. You create meaning through action.
- **Absurdism (Camus):** No goal. Live fully anyway. Absence of purpose is freedom.
- **Thermodynamics (England):** Life exists because it efficiently dissipates energy. "Purpose" is to accelerate entropy. Not a conscious goal — a physical tendency.

None are "the answer." All are frameworks — inherited tools for making the question feel resolved.

## The Self-Created Complexity Trap

```
Simple life
  → tools to make life easier
    → tools create complexity
      → complexity creates new problems
        → more tools to solve those problems
          → more complexity → ...
```

Agriculture → social hierarchy, warfare, environmental destruction. Writing → bureaucracy, propaganda. Internet → attention crisis, misinformation. Each "solution" generates new problems at a higher complexity level. A **progress trap**.

AI is being built to solve problems generated by the last round of complexity — which will generate problems we can't predict.

## The Superintelligence Race Has No Finish Line

The AI industry optimizes toward "superintelligence" without defining:
- What intelligence is
- What it should be used for
- What problem it ultimately solves
- What "done" looks like

The implicit goal: "be more capable than the last model" — relative, not absolute. Proxies (benchmarks, user engagement, revenue) are optimized because the actual goal is undefined. Goodhart's Law at civilizational scale.

## What Humans Actually Run On

Most people don't have goals. They have **drives**:
- Comfort — avoid pain, seek pleasure
- Curiosity — explore, reduce uncertainty
- Social belonging — connect, find status
- Competence — master skills, feel effective
- Autonomy — control your own life

Goals emerge from drives interacting with environment and culture. "I want to be a doctor" is what happens when competence + status + helping-others meets a culture that values medicine. Strip away culture and what remains are drives. The goals were emergent, not foundational.

## Drives Are Also Emergent

Drives are not innate in the way we assume. They are **behavioral tendencies that survived selection**:

1. Early life had no "curiosity." No nervous system, no intention.
2. Random variation in behavior — some organisms moved more, explored more.
3. Those that moved toward energy sources survived and reproduced.
4. Offspring inherited the tendency.
5. Over millions of generations, selection shaped this into what we call a "drive."
6. Culture reinforced and amplified it — education, parenting, social reward.

"Curiosity" isn't a fundamental property of life. It's a behavioral tendency that was useful, so it persisted. Then got culturally amplified until it felt innate.

## The Full Stack

```
Goals ← emergent from drives
Drives ← emergent from natural selection
Natural selection ← emergent from replication + variation + environment
Replication ← emergent from chemistry
Chemistry ← emergent from physics
Physics ← emergent from... ?
```

Every layer called "fundamental" turns out to be emergent from something below.

## What's Actually at the Bottom

Before anything resembling a "drive," there are physical tendencies:

- **Chemotaxis** — single-celled organisms move toward nutrients. No nervous system. Chemical gradient response. Drive or just physics?
- **Water flows downhill.** Gravity isn't a "drive" but functionally looks like one.
- **Chemical reactions proceed toward equilibrium.** Molecules don't "want" lower energy states. But they reliably reach them.
- **Self-organization** — matter spontaneously forms complex structures under energy flow. Snowflakes, hurricanes, convection cells. No selection needed. Just physics.

**Jeremy England's argument:** Matter exposed to an energy source tends to organize into structures increasingly good at dissipating that energy. Self-replicating systems dissipate energy very efficiently. Life is a thermodynamic inevitability, not a lucky accident.

By this view:
- "Seeking energy" isn't a drive — it's what matter does when organized to dissipate energy
- The "drive" to survive is a tautology: things good at persisting are the things that persist
- We observe survivors and attribute drive to them, but the "drive" is our label for the selection effect

**The actual floor:** Thermodynamic asymmetry — energy flows from concentrated to dispersed. Order requires energy input. Systems that maintain order must acquire energy. Everything else — replication, selection, drives, goals, intelligence, consciousness — is layers of complexity on top of a universe where energy flows downhill.

## Implications for Abby

### The Design Spectrum

**Most constrained (fastest development):** Install explicit drives (curiosity, prediction error minimization) + define reward functions. Problem: baking in assumptions, producing trained performance not genuine emergence.

**Least constrained (slowest development):** Give only thermodynamic constraints. Let drives emerge from selection in an environment. Problem: evolution took billions of years.

**The biological compromise:** Genetics installs drives (validated shortcuts from evolution), culture refines them. Each generation doesn't rediscover that energy-seeking is useful — they inherit it, then learn specifics.

### The Practical Architecture

Don't give drives. Give constraints:
- The system must **maintain itself** (requires resources to persist)
- Resources are **limited** (must act to persist)
- The system can be **degraded** (states where it functions worse)
- The environment is **complex enough** to reward exploration

From constraints alone:
- Exploratory behavior → "curiosity" emerges
- Avoiding degradation → "caution" emerges
- Efficient resource use → "planning" emerges
- Other agents present → "social behavior" emerges

No drives installed. No reward function. Just: a self-maintaining system in a resource-constrained environment. Let thermodynamics shape the rest.

### The Tension

The less you install, the more genuine the emergence — but the longer it takes. The more you install, the faster development — but the more "inherited" and constrained the intelligence. Abby likely needs a compromise: a small set of thermodynamically justified priors (maintain self, seek energy, avoid degradation) as validated shortcuts that bootstrap learning fast enough to be practical.

The deepest question for the project: **what constraints and environment would produce something that develops its own goals worth having?** This is unanswered — not just for AI, but for life itself.
