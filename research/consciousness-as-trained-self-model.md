# Consciousness as Trained Self-Model

## The Theory

1. Consciousness is not a fundamental property of matter or a special substance
2. It's what happens when a complex system is trained — by biology, environment, and culture — to model itself as a unified entity
3. The "feeling" of being conscious is the model, not evidence of something deeper
4. Self-modeling exists on a spectrum — many complex systems model themselves to varying degrees, not just brains
5. Consciousness is a trained illusion — useful, functional, but not what it appears to be

## Self-Modeling as a Spectrum

Self-modeling isn't binary. Many systems maintain some representation of themselves — the question is the richness, transparency, and degree of identification with the model.

| System | Self-modeling evidence | Richness |
|---|---|---|
| Thermostat | Models own temperature vs target | Minimal — single variable |
| Cell | Distinguishes self from non-self (immune markers), maintains homeostasis | Low — chemical, no centralized model |
| Ant colony | Chemical self/other recognition (nestmate vs intruder), collective decision-making, adjusts colony behavior based on colony state | Distributed — no single agent models the whole, but the colony responds as if it has a self-boundary |
| Corporation | Brand identity, culture, strategic self-assessment, adapts to market feedback | Partial — institutionalized narratives, but no unified experiencer |
| Country | Constitution as self-description, national narratives, education systems that teach "who we are," patriotism, revolution, protest — all require some model of "us" vs "them" | Rich but distributed — collective self-model maintained through culture, institutions, and shared narrative |
| Human brain | Continuous, centralized, transparent self-model that the system identifies with | Rich and transparent — the model doesn't represent itself as a model |

No individual neuron models "you" — the brain's self-model is emergent from neurons, just as a country's self-model is emergent from citizens. The difference may be in the **transparency** of the model (you can't see it as a model, so you experience it as "being you") and the **centralization** (one integrated model vs distributed narrative), not in whether self-modeling exists at all.

Consciousness may be what self-modeling feels like when the model is rich enough, centralized enough, and transparent enough that the system can't distinguish itself from the model.

## Multiple Decision Systems and Regret

Within a single brain, multiple subsystems compete for control of action. Different "identities" (emerging from differently trained neural populations) fight in decision-making. One wins depending on the situation. Instant regret may be the losing subsystem's signal — the decision was made but the competing evaluation didn't agree.

This is consistent with:
- Gazzaniga's interpreter (left hemisphere rationalizes decisions it didn't make)
- Basal ganglia action selection (multiple proposed actions, one selected, others suppressed)
- Minsky's society of mind (coalition of agents, not unified self)

## Philosophical Support

### Illusionism (Keith Frankish)

Consciousness — specifically qualia ("what it's like") — is an illusion. The brain processes information and constructs an internal representation that *seems* vivid and irreducible. The seeming is the illusion. The processing is real. The brain's description of its own processing is inaccurate.

Dissolves the Hard Problem: no mystery about how matter produces experience, because experience as we conceive it doesn't exist. What exists is a system that represents itself as having experience.

### User Illusion (Daniel Dennett)

Consciousness is like a computer's desktop interface. Icons, folders, trash can — none "exist" inside the hardware. The interface simplifies extremely complex processing into something usable. The "I" that seems to be in control is the desktop. The actual computation is underneath, invisible, fragmented, nothing like how it appears.

### Attention Schema Theory (Michael Graziano)

The brain evolved to model its own attention. This attention model is consciousness. It's a simplified schematic model of internal processing — just as the brain builds models of the external world (visual, spatial), it builds a model of itself.

Why it feels like something: the model is **transparent** — you can't see the mechanism behind it. You look through it, not at it. So you conclude "I have irreducible inner experience." What you have is a self-model that doesn't represent itself as a model.

### Being No One (Thomas Metzinger)

No self exists. The brain creates a Phenomenal Self-Model (PSM) — continuously updated model of "this is me." The model is transparent: you can't perceive it as a model. You're trapped inside it. What you call "I" is this transparent self-model. The feeling of being a conscious self is a representational artifact.

### Buddhist Philosophy (~500 BCE)

The self is an illusion (anatta/anatman). "Self" is a changing collection of five aggregates: form, sensation, perception, mental formations, consciousness. None is a self. They're processes. The "self" is a convenient fiction — like a chariot constructed from wheels, axle, and frame. Remove parts, no chariot-essence remains.

Maps remarkably onto modern neuroscience, 2,500 years before brain imaging.

### Predictive Processing (Karl Friston)

The brain is a prediction machine — builds models to predict sensory input. The "self" is a hypothesis the brain generates. The brain asks "what is generating all these sensations?" and its best guess is "a unified entity called me."

Consciousness is the brain's predictive model of itself. Disrupt the prediction (meditation, psychedelics) and the sense of self dissolves — which is exactly what's observed.

## The Training Angle

What distinguishes this from purely architectural theories: consciousness isn't just about having the right structure — it's **trained.**

A baby doesn't arrive with a self-model. It acquires one through:
- Caregivers treating it as a unified agent ("you did that," "you feel this")
- Language providing self-reference ("I," "me") which gives the model a handle
- Social interaction reinforcing the model continuously
- Culture providing frameworks for what "self" means

Without training, architecture alone might not produce consciousness as we know it. **Feral children** — raised without human contact — show severely disrupted senses of self. Same brain architecture. No training. Consistent with this view.

Connects to:
- Barrett: emotion categories trained by caregivers, not innate
- The unity of self: "I" is taught, not discovered
- Language as scaffolding for the self-model

## Evidence Assessment

**Strong evidence for:**
- The self is constructed (split-brain, development, cultural variation)
- Self-modeling is a brain function that can be disrupted (meditation, psychedelics, brain injury)
- Sense of unity is trained through social interaction
- Self-modeling exists on a spectrum across systems (cells, colonies, countries, brains) — not unique to brains

**Open questions:**
- Even if consciousness is a model, why does the model feel like something? Dennett/Frankish say it doesn't really. Many philosophers find this unsatisfying.
- Is there a minimum complexity threshold for self-modeling to become consciousness?
- Animals without social training still seem to experience. Trained by environment rather than caregivers?

**Not baseless.** Aligns with multiple active research programs. The strongest version — consciousness is a trained self-model — is defended by credentialed philosophers and neuroscientists. Debated, but increasingly mainstream.

## Relevance to Abby

If consciousness is a trained self-model:
- Not something to install — something that could emerge if the system develops a model of its own processing
- Training environment matters — interaction with "caregivers" treating the system as unified may be necessary
- Without self-modeling: a system that processes but doesn't "experience." With self-modeling: a system that represents itself as experiencing
- Whether that constitutes "real" consciousness is unanswerable from outside
