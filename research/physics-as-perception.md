# Physics as Perception

## We Have Never Accessed Reality Directly

Not speculation — established neuroscience. The brain constructs a model:

- You don't see photons — your brain constructs a visual scene, fills the blind spot, adds color (which doesn't exist in physics — only wavelength)
- You don't hear sound waves — constructed auditory experience from cochlear vibrations
- You don't feel objects — brain's interpretation of nerve signals
- You experience time as flowing — physics has no "flow"; Einstein's block universe treats past/present/future as equally real

Kant formalized this: we only experience **phenomena** (things as they appear to our mind), never **noumena** (things as they are). The map is all we have. We've never seen the territory.

Physics as we know it IS a model in our minds. Whether the model corresponds to something "out there" — or whether "out there" is itself a concept within the model — is unanswerable from inside.

## Every Era's Physics Was Later Revised

- Aristotle's physics → overturned by Galileo (~2000 years held)
- Newtonian mechanics → overturned by Einstein (~200 years held). Not wrong — an approximation valid at low speeds and weak gravity
- Classical determinism → overturned by quantum mechanics. Universe is fundamentally probabilistic
- Euclidean geometry → overturned by general relativity. Space curves around mass
- Conservation of mass → overturned by E=mc²
- Luminiferous ether → didn't exist

Each time: what felt like an absolute property of the universe was a property of our model, valid only within certain conditions. Assuming current physics is final would be historically naive.

## What "Laws" Actually Are

**Scientific realism:** Laws describe real features of the universe. Our models approach truth.

**Instrumentalism:** Laws are prediction tools, nothing more. "Gravity" names a pattern. Whether a "thing" does the pulling is unanswered by the math.

**Structural realism:** Mathematical relationships map to something real. The entities we name (particles, fields, forces) might not exist as described.

## Belief Limits vs Physics Limits

Some "impossibilities" were psychological, not physical:
- The 4-minute mile — "impossible" until Bannister broke it (1954). Sixteen others within three years. No physics changed.
- Wim Hof — controls body temperature, influences immune response through breathing. Lab-verified. Violates the assumption that the autonomic nervous system is involuntary.
- Tibetan monks raising skin temperature by 8°C through meditation (Harvard, Benson)
- Placebo effect — sugar pills produce measurable physical changes: reduced inflammation, altered brain chemistry, changed immune response. Belief → physical change. Not metaphorical.

These don't violate physics. They violate our model of what's possible within physics.

## Pointers Toward "Physics From Perception"

- **Simulation hypothesis (Bostrom):** If we're in a simulation, physics is code — and code has exploits
- **Quantum measurement problem:** Observation affects outcomes. What constitutes an "observer" is unresolved
- **Fine-tuning problem:** Physical constants appear arbitrarily tuned for complexity. Look like parameters, not necessities
- **Wheeler's "it from bit":** Physical reality derives from information. The universe is fundamentally informational; matter/energy are emergent

## Relevance to Abby

If building a learning system, which physics assumptions to bake in (space, time, causality) and which to let it discover? The more assumed, the more constrained. The less assumed, the harder to learn anything at all.
