# Intelligence

## The Problem with Common Definitions

Intelligence is typically defined as cognition, problem-solving ability, or "figuring out what to do to be successful." These are very different things, and all are circular — they measure how good an entity is at goals *humans* decided matter.

- Nobel laureates are called intelligent because they solved hard problems. But "hard" and "worth solving" are human judgements, not universal truths.
- Millionaires are called intelligent, but money is a human-contrived construct. No one knows if accumulating wealth is objectively worthwhile.
- A person who hasn't read widely or done formal research may be extraordinarily effective at reading people, maintaining relationships, raising children (literally building new minds). No test measures this. No institution rewards it.

## Intelligence is Subjective and Contextual

Intelligence is subjective to each individual because it depends on their belief system — what they consider important, hard, and worth spending time on. That belief system is itself shaped by environment:

1. **Environment shapes belief system** → what you think is important, hard, worth doing
2. **Belief system defines "intelligence"** → you call people intelligent who are good at what you value
3. **This is a feedback loop, not a fixed trait**

A fish is more intelligent than Einstein — in water. A cockroach has survived 300 million years. Ranking these on a single scale is a category error.

## Intelligence is Not a Property of an Entity

Intelligence is a property of the **relationship between an entity and its environment**, not of the entity alone. A brain in a jar isn't intelligent — it has no environment to be intelligent relative to.

Even within the same environment, two humans perceive different things as complex or important based on their accumulated belief systems. So intelligence is:
- Entity-relative
- Environment-relative
- Perspective-relative (within the same entity-environment pair)

## Connection to Emergence

Interactions between entities create emergent complexity. An entity embedded in these interactions needs to navigate that complexity to survive or benefit. What "navigating well" looks like depends on the specific complexity that emerged. This creates another feedback loop:

- Interactions produce emergent complexity
- Entities must understand and navigate that complexity
- What counts as "intelligent navigation" is defined by the complexity itself
- The entity's responses create new interactions, which produce new complexity

Intelligence can't be separated from the emergent system it's embedded in. See [[emergence]].

## Implications for Abby

You can't build "general intelligence" without also building the environment it's embedded in and the value system that defines what matters. The system and its context are inseparable — they must be co-designed. An artificial learner without an environment to be intelligent *relative to* is meaningless.
