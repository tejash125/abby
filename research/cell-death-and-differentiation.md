# Cell Death and Differentiation

## Why Cells Die

### Apoptosis — Programmed Self-Destruction

Cells are programmed to kill themselves when damaged, old, or no longer needed. Active process requiring energy. The cell dismantles itself in orderly fashion: DNA cut into fragments, organelles packaged into neat bundles (apoptotic bodies), neighbors and immune cells clean up. No mess, no inflammation.

**Scale:** ~50-70 billion cells die by apoptosis every day in an adult human.

**Triggers:**
- Internal: DNA damage beyond repair, protein misfolding, oxidative stress, telomere exhaustion
- External: immune cells signaling death, loss of survival signals from neighbors

**Essential for development:**
- Fingers carved from mitten-shaped hand by killing webbing cells
- T-cells that would attack own body killed during immune development
- Gut lining replaces itself every 3-5 days

**Cancer is fundamentally a failure of apoptosis.** Cells that broke their self-destruct machinery.

### Telomere Shortening — The Replication Counter

Telomeres: protective caps on chromosome ends. Shorten with each division. After ~50-70 divisions (Hayflick limit), too short → cell enters senescence or triggers apoptosis.

Built-in replication counter. Cancer cells hack it by reactivating **telomerase** (enzyme that rebuilds telomeres) → unlimited division.

### Senescence — Alive But Retired

Cells stop dividing but don't die. Secrete inflammatory signals (SASP). Body normally clears them with immune cells, but cleanup degrades with age. Accumulation of senescent cells is a primary mechanism of aging.

Active research: **senolytics** — drugs that selectively kill senescent cells. Some rejuvenation effects in animal models.

### Necrosis — Unplanned Death

Cell death from injury, infection, toxins, oxygen deprivation. Cell swells and bursts, contents spill. Triggers inflammation. Messy. What happens in heart attacks.

## Stem Cells and Differentiation

### The Fundamental Fact

Every cell in the body has the **same complete DNA.** What makes a liver cell different from a neuron is which genes are active — not which genes exist.

### How Differentiation Works

DNA as a book with 20,000 chapters. Stem cell: all chapters open. As it differentiates, chapters are selectively activated or silenced. Silencing becomes increasingly permanent.

The machinery (**epigenetics**):
- **DNA methylation** — methyl groups silence genes. Like gluing pages shut.
- **Histone modification** — changing how tightly DNA is wound. Tight = gene off. Loose = gene on.
- **Transcription factors** — proteins that activate specific genes. Keys for specific locks.

### Stem Cell Potency Spectrum

```
Totipotent → Pluripotent → Multipotent → Unipotent → Fully differentiated

+ placenta)    cell)        related)      only)
```

## Can Cells Change Their Function?

### Normally: Almost Never

Fully differentiated cells are locked. Epigenetic modifications are stable and self-reinforcing. By design — chaos if cells randomly switched.

### Exceptions

**Transdifferentiation (natural):** Direct conversion, rare in mammals.
- Salamanders: pigmented eye cells → lens cells
- Liver: hepatocytes ↔ bile duct cells under injury
- Pancreas: acinar → beta-like cells under stress

**Dedifferentiation:** Specialized cell reverts to stem-like state.
- Salamander limb regeneration: mature cells → blastema (stem-like mass) → redifferentiate into all needed types
- Very limited in mammals. Why salamanders can and we can't is an open question — we have the same genes, ours are more tightly locked.

**Metaplasia:** Tissue type converts under chronic irritation.
- Barrett's esophagus: acid reflux → esophageal cells become intestinal-like
- Smoker's lungs: ciliated cells → squamous cells
- Often pre-cancerous

**Induced Pluripotent Stem Cells — Yamanaka, 2006 (Nobel Prize 2012):**
Fully differentiated adult skin cell → reprogrammed back to pluripotent state using just four transcription factors (Oct4, Sox2, Klf4, c-Myc). Resets epigenetic state. Un-glues the chapters. The resulting iPSC can become any cell type.

**What this proved:** Differentiation is NOT permanent. The information to be any cell is always there in every cell, just silenced. Cell identity is a **state**, not a permanent change.

**Direct Reprogramming (skip stem cell step):**
- Skin cells → neurons (Wernig, 2010)
- Skin cells → heart cells
- Skin cells → liver cells
- Uses specific transcription factor cocktails per conversion

### The Key Insight

Cell identity is epigenetic state, not permanent structure. Every cell carries complete instructions to be any other cell. The difference is which pages are bookmarked and which are glued shut. The glue can be dissolved. Identity is configuration, not hardware.
