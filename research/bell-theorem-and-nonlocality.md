# What Bell's Theorem Actually Proves

This is the most misstated result in physics. Most textbooks say it "rules out hidden variables" or "forces you to give up realism." Both are wrong. It rules out **locality**. Getting the logic exactly right is the whole point.

## The one word everything hinges on

**Locality** = causes and effects spread no faster than light. Nothing here can instantly affect something over there; influence has to travel. Einstein needed this because without it, observers moving at different speeds disagree about which event caused which, and cause-and-effect itself breaks down (see [[speed-of-light-and-measurement]]). Fixing exactly this non-locality in Newton's gravity is what drove him to general relativity — gravity as ripples in spacetime spreading at light speed, not instant action at a distance.

## The chain of reasoning

1. **EPR (1935).** Make two entangled particles with opposite spin. Quantum mechanics says neither has a definite spin until measured — and the instant you measure one, the other's spin is fixed, however far away. EPR's point: that is non-local. So either nature really is non-local (Einstein: unacceptable), **or** the particles secretly agreed on their answers before separating — sealed "envelopes" they carry. Those pre-agreed answers are **hidden variables**, and because they're fixed while the particles are still together, it is a *local* hidden variable theory.

2. **The stalemate.** For the basic experiment, quantum mechanics and local-hidden-variables predict *identical* results. Untestable, so it was dismissed as philosophy and ignored for ~30 years.

3. **Bell's twist (1964).** Let each experimenter independently pick one of three measurement angles (0°, 120°, 240°). When they pick *different* angles, how often do their results disagree? Quantum mechanics predicts **25%**. Any possible local-hidden-variable strategy — particles deciding in advance how to answer all three questions — is forced to disagree **at least 33%** of the time. Pre-agreed answers can't help matching too well. Different number, so now it's testable.

4. **The experiment (Aspect and others).** Result: 25%. Quantum mechanics wins.

5. **The conclusion, read backwards.** We *assumed* locality → that forced local hidden variables → Bell showed local hidden variables give the wrong number → therefore **the locality assumption is false.** The result is **non-locality, full stop.**

## What it does NOT prove

Hidden variables are fine — they just have to be **non-local**. Pilot-wave (Bohmian) mechanics is exactly a non-local hidden variable theory, and Bell defended it his whole life. "Local realism" is a confused phrase; the thing that dies is locality alone. Bell himself called the textbook "rules out hidden variables" claim a remarkable, widespread error.

## Why the non-locality doesn't blow up the universe

If influences really beat light speed, why no time-travel paradoxes? Because each individual outcome is **random**. You get plus-or-minus at random; so does your partner. The two are *correlated*, but you can't *use* the correlation to send a message — you only see it by comparing both records afterward, which travels normally. So it violates the *spirit* of relativity (something non-local is going on) without breaking the *letter* (no faster-than-light signaling). An uneasy truce.

This is the same shape as the entanglement-as-conservation point in [[photon-entanglement]]: the correlation is real and absolute, the individual values are not decided until measured, and nothing is actually transmitted.

## The escape hatch: many worlds

Drop collapse entirely. Both outcomes happen; reality branches; you split into two versions, each seeing one result. Bell's proof *assumed each measurement has a single outcome* — false in many worlds — so the proof doesn't even apply. The correlation needs no faster-than-light phone call because both answers already exist in the branching structure. This recovers locality in the sense that matters (no faster-than-light *influence*), at the price of infinite copies of you. Its appeal isn't just locality; it also removes the arbitrary "what counts as a measurement?" line that the collapse picture never explains.

## The thread that matters most

The deepest line in the source: *"it's measurements that are the problem in quantum mechanics."* The non-locality lives entirely in collapse-at-measurement. Reality doesn't hand you a definite value — the act of measuring produces one. That is the same structure as observer-applied categories elsewhere (see [[physics-as-perception]] and [[double-slit-experiment]]): the definite value is created at the interface, not read off a world that already had it.

## Open questions

- Is the right reading "nature is non-local" or "collapse is the wrong picture" (many worlds / pilot wave)? The experiments don't decide between non-local interpretations — they only kill *locality with single outcomes*.
- If many worlds restores locality, the decades-long failure to unify quantum mechanics with gravity might be because we kept trying to marry a local relativity to a non-local quantum theory. A local quantum theory would change that problem.
