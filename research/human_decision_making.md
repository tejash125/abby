# Human Decision Making

## Neuron Structure

The basic building blocks of decision-making hardware:

| Component | What it does | Key detail |
|-----------|-------------|------------|
| **Dendrites** | Short branching "antennae" that receive chemical signals from other neurons and funnel electrical changes toward the soma | A neuron can have thousands of dendritic branches — a large "listening surface" |
| **Soma (cell body)** | Integrates all incoming dendritic signals; fires an action potential if voltage crosses threshold | The neuron's command center and metabolic engine |
| **Axon** (with myelin + nodes of Ranvier) | Carries the all-or-nothing electrical impulse away from the soma toward other cells | Myelin insulation lets signals jump node-to-node, making conduction up to 100x faster |
| **Axon terminals** | Convert electrical impulse into chemical form — release neurotransmitter across a synapse to the next cell | One axon can split into hundreds of terminals, broadcasting widely |
| **Oligodendrocyte** (glial cell) | Produces the myelin sheath; one can insulate segments on dozens of neighbouring axons | Damage leads to demyelinating diseases like MS |

## Decision-Making Hierarchy (Fastest to Slowest)

The brain has a layered system for decision-making, from pure reflex to extended deliberation:

### 0. Monosynaptic spinal reflexes (20-50ms)
Withdrawal and postural "no-brainers." One sensory synapse onto motor neurons in the spinal cord. No cortex involvement at all.

### 1. Trans-cortical & brain-stem sensorimotor loops (70-100ms)
Fast corrective movements, startle, vestibulo-ocular reflex. Spinal cord, cerebellum, and primary motor cortex adjust the reflex before conscious thought.

### 2. Rapid affective appraisal — "low-road" emotion (120-250ms)
Threat/salience detection. Superior colliculus and pulvinar deliver coarse sensory gist straight to the amygdala, priming fight-or-flight before you "know" what you saw.

### 3. Habit / procedural controller (200-500ms)
Well-learned stimulus-response chains ("autopilot"). Loops through basal ganglia, cerebellum, and premotor cortex gate actions without full cortical deliberation.

### 4. Perceptual evidence accumulator — "System 1" intuition (0.3-1s)
Choosing between competing options as evidence builds. Parietal cortex implements a drift-diffusion-like race to a decision bound.

### 5. Working-memory & rule-guided decisions (1-10s)
Hold several items/rules, re-evaluate, inhibit impulses. Prefrontal cortex oscillations maintain information over seconds.

### 6. Multi-step analytical reasoning — "System 2" (10s-minutes)
Planning, algebra, weighing pros/cons. Anterior PFC, hippocampus, and parietal cortex pass partial results back and forth across sub-decisions.

### 7. Extended deliberation & mind-wandering (minutes-hours)
Creative synthesis, life planning, narrative thinking. Default-mode network pools information across whole episodes.

### 8. Neuromodulatory & hormonal regulators (hours-days)
Dopamine, norepinephrine, serotonin, and endocrine signals bias the entire ladder toward vigilance, hunger, sleep, etc. Not a decision layer itself — it tunes the gain and thresholds for every faster layer.

## Key Observations

- Decision-making is not one process — it's at least 9 distinct systems operating at different timescales, from 20ms reflexes to multi-day hormonal regulation
- Faster layers can act before slower layers even engage — you flinch before you understand why
- Each layer can override or modulate the layers below it (System 2 suppressing a reflex) and above it (hormones biasing deliberation)
- This is a hierarchical system but not a clean one — layers interact, compete, and sometimes conflict

## Relevance to Abby

This hierarchy suggests a learning system needs multiple timescales of processing, not a single unified decision loop. Fast pattern-matching (reflexes, System 1) and slow deliberation (System 2) appear to be fundamentally different mechanisms, not just the same process running longer.
