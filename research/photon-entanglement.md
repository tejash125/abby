# Photon Entanglement

## What a photon is

Light is energy traveling through space. A photon is the smallest possible amount of light — you can't have half a photon. It's the minimum unit.

A photon has two key properties:
- **Energy** — which we perceive as color. High energy = blue/violet. Low energy = red. Higher still = ultraviolet (invisible). Lower still = infrared (invisible). Energy and color are the same thing.
- **Direction** — it's moving somewhere.

## How light interacts with materials

### Absorption (opaque materials)

A photon hits an atom, and the atom swallows it entirely. The photon's energy kicks one of the atom's electrons to a higher energy state. This only works if the photon's energy **exactly matches** a gap between energy states the electron can be in — like a staircase where the electron can stand on step 1 or step 2, but not 1.5.

After absorption, the electron is in an unstable high state. It must come back down. Several options:
- Drop back down the same gap → emit a photon with the same energy, but in a random direction
- Drop down in multiple smaller steps → emit multiple lower-energy photons
- Transfer energy to neighboring atoms as vibration (heat) → no photon comes out
- Mix of heat + lower-energy photon (fluorescence)

In a closed system, total energy is always conserved. If a lower-energy photon comes out, the "missing" energy is in the material as heat.

This is why glass is transparent to visible light but opaque to ultraviolet — visible photon energies don't match any gaps in glass's structure, so they pass through. Ultraviolet photons do match, so they're absorbed.

### Passing through (transparent materials)

The photon's energy doesn't match any gap, so no absorption occurs. Instead, the photon's oscillating electric field makes electrons in the material **wobble in place** — they don't jump to a higher state, just vibrate in response. Their wobbling modifies how the light wave propagates, slowing it down. Light in glass moves at about 2/3 its vacuum speed, in water about 3/4, in diamond about 2/5.

The photon exits with **the same energy** (same color). It just arrives later. No energy was lost.

This is fundamentally different from absorption — no electron transitions, no energy transfer, just a temporary interaction that slows the wave down.

## How entangled photon pairs are created

### The crystal process

Certain crystals have an **asymmetric** atomic arrangement — the structure looks different depending on which direction you look at it. The most commonly used is beta barium borate (barium, boron, and oxygen atoms in a specific pattern).

When a photon enters this crystal, very rarely — roughly one in a billion times — the interaction between the photon and the crystal's asymmetric structure causes the original photon to **cease to exist** and two new photons to **come into existence**.

This is not "splitting" — a photon is the minimum unit and can't be cut. The original is destroyed, two new ones are created. The crystal mediates this but doesn't gain or lose energy — it's the environment where the conversion happens.

Two unbreakable rules constrain the two new photons:

**Energy conservation:** Their combined energies must equal the original photon's energy. A blue (high energy) photon in → two red (lower energy) photons out.

**Momentum conservation:** Their combined directions must match the original photon's direction. If one goes slightly left, the other must go slightly right by exactly the compensating amount.

### Why the two photons are entangled

The conservation rules say the totals must add up, but they don't specify how the total is divided. Maybe photon A gets 40% of the energy and B gets 60%. Or 50/50. Or 30/70.

According to the experimental evidence (Bell tests), the individual values are **not determined until one is measured**. But the constraint "A + B = total" is absolute. So when you measure A and find 40, B instantly becomes 60 — not because A sent a message to B, but because they were always two parts of a single conserved quantity that only resolves into specific parts upon measurement.

### The crystal isn't the only method

The original entanglement experiments (1972, 1982) didn't use crystals. They used individual calcium atoms excited so that an electron jumped up **two** energy steps. When the electron fell back down in two stages, it emitted two photons — one per step. Those two photons were entangled, constrained by the same conservation laws.

Entanglement isn't a property of crystals or special equipment. It's a consequence of **any process where two particles are created from a single event and conservation laws constrain their combined properties.**

Ordinary light sources (light bulbs, the sun) don't produce entangled photons because each photon is emitted independently by a separate atom. No shared event, no conservation constraint linking them.

## How entanglement was proven real

### Einstein's objection (1935)

Einstein argued this prediction was absurd. His reasoning:
1. Nothing can influence something else faster than light
2. If measuring particle A instantly determines particle B's state across any distance, that would require faster-than-light influence
3. Therefore, the particles must have had definite properties all along — we just didn't know them
4. There must be hidden predetermined information decided when the particles were created

For ~30 years, this was a philosophical argument with no way to settle it.

### Bell's test (1964)

John Bell figured out how to experimentally distinguish between the two explanations. If you measure the two linked particles at various different angles (not just the same angle every time), the pattern of correlations will be statistically different depending on which explanation is correct.

He derived a mathematical limit: if correlations stay below it, hidden predetermined information works. If they exceed it, only the "genuinely undetermined until measured" explanation works.

### The experiments

**1972 — Freedman and Clauser (UC Berkeley):** Created entangled photon pairs from calcium atoms, measured at different angles. Correlations exceeded Bell's limit. Hidden information explanation didn't fit.

**1982 — Aspect (Paris):** Closed the biggest loophole — changed the measurement angle while photons were already in flight, faster than light could travel between detectors. No possible communication between them. Still exceeded Bell's limit.

**2015 and beyond:** Systematically closed every remaining proposed loophole. Same results every time.

**2022 — Nobel Prize** awarded to Clauser, Aspect, and Zeilinger for these experiments.

## What entanglement actually is — beyond conservation arithmetic

Conservation arithmetic (A + B = total, individual values undecided until measurement) is the surface description. The deeper claim: for two entangled particles, **you cannot describe each particle separately.** The pair has a description, but neither individual particle has its own complete description.

This is different from classical ignorance. If you put a red ball in one box and blue in another, each ball **has** a definite color — you just don't know which. The ball in New York is red before you open the box. Your ignorance doesn't change the ball.

Entanglement is NOT this. The claim — backed by experiments — is that each particle genuinely does not have a definite individual state. Only the pair as a whole has a definite state. The individual particles aren't "unknown red" or "unknown blue" — the question "what color is this one?" doesn't have an answer yet.

## Alternative explanations the experiments do NOT rule out

The Bell tests conclusively rule out one specific alternative: **local hidden variables** (each particle carries its own predetermined information, nothing communicates faster than light). The correlations are too strong — strongest experiments exceed Bell's ceiling by 244 standard deviations.

But other alternatives remain viable:

**Non-local hidden variables (Bohm, 1952):** Particles DO have definite positions at all times, but are guided by a "pilot wave" that connects them across any distance instantly. Measurement of one updates the pilot wave, instantly affecting the other. Reproduces all experimental results. Most physicists don't use it because it requires accepting instantaneous influence across the universe — but "feels uncomfortable" isn't a scientific argument.

**Superdeterminism:** Nothing in the universe is random. The experimenters' "choice" of measurement angle, the particles' behavior — all determined from the beginning of the universe. Can't be disproven by definition. Bell himself acknowledged this loophole.

**Retrocausality:** Influence travels backward in time. Measuring particle A now sends influence back to the moment both particles were created, setting up the correlation from the start. Taken seriously by some physicists. Not testable in any obvious way.

**Non-separability:** "Two particles" is the wrong description. What we call two entangled particles is actually one thing that we force into a "two separate objects" framework because it's in two places. Entanglement is what happens when we impose the label "separate" on something that isn't.

All of these reproduce the same experimental data. They differ in what they claim about reality, not in what they predict you'll observe.

## What's established vs what's open

**Established (not seriously disputed):**
- Entangled particle pairs can be created and detected
- Their correlations exceed what's possible if properties were predetermined without faster-than-light connection (Bell tests)
- This works regardless of distance between particles
- No faster-than-light communication is possible using entanglement (individual results look random from one side alone — the pattern only appears when you compare both sides)

**Open (genuinely unresolved):**
- Does measuring one particle *cause* something to happen to the other? Or is the correlation a property of how the pair was created?
- Is the phenomenon best described as instant influence, pilot waves, non-separability, retrocausality, or something we don't have a concept for yet?
- "Separate" and "independent" may be labels that work at our scale but don't apply at this level — the two particles might not be two separate things in the way we assume
