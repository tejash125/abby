# Experiment 1 — The Foundations of Learning

## Status

**Hypothesis stage. No code yet.** We are pinning down, in plain English, the assumptions we make about how learning works at all, before deciding what (if anything) to build. Per project convention, code only follows an idea file.

## What this experiment is about

The question: *what are the minimal things a thing needs in order to learn anything at all?*

This file holds the philosophy we are **assuming** — our own first-principles thinking, not something summarized from an outside source. (Knowledge taken from outside goes in `research/`. The hypotheses we are assuming live here.)

We reason as if we are the first minds ever asking how learning happens, taking nothing about computers, brains, or existing AI for granted.

## The hypothesis: learning has two engines, joined by trust

### Engine 1 — the world teaches you (regularities)

The world has patterns. A learner with a boundary, contact with the world (senses), and a trace that lasts (memory) can do the most basic thing there is: **expect what comes next**, then notice when it was **wrong**, then **adjust** so it is less wrong next time. No teacher is needed, because the world itself reveals the answer a moment later and grades the guess for free. This engine catches **regularities** — patterns that are out there whether or not anyone agrees on them.

### Engine 2 — a trusted other teaches you (conventions)

Much of what a learner ends up knowing was never discovered from the world directly. It was **handed over** by someone, and accepted **without checking**, because the source was trusted. A child does not discover "plus" by watching the world; they are told it, and they build on top of it. Knowledge is a castle: each floor is something we agreed to assume so we could build the next floor. This engine carries **conventions** — the agreed parts (names, directions, where-to-start assumptions) that the world alone can never teach.

### Trust is the hinge

The two engines are not separate. The world (Engine 1) can teach regularities but can never teach conventions — no amount of watching the sky tells you to call one side "east." Conventions can only arrive through a source you accept without verifying, and accepting-without-verifying is what **trust** is.

How does a fresh learner decide who to trust? Through Engine 1: one source in its world is unusually consistent, responsive, and tied to its needs being met. A source that Engine 1 finds reliable becomes trusted. So **Engine 1 builds trust, and trust opens Engine 2.** The pattern-catcher bootstraps the convention-adopter.

And understanding happens where the two meet: a convention adopted through trust becomes real *understanding* only when it gets anchored to regularities the learner also caught on its own. Told-without-grounding gives you a child reciting the times-table like a song; told-and-grounded gives you a child who can do a sum it never saw.

### The floor: there are no facts

Every castle, including math and physics, bottoms out in assumptions, not proofs. Any attempt to prove something either runs on forever, loops, or stops at something simply assumed. So there is no bedrock of facts; it is agreements all the way down.

But not all agreements are equal: **the world votes.** The agreements that keep correctly anticipating what happens are the ones that survive. A "fact" like "the sun rises in the east" is a stubborn regularity (the bright thing reliably comes up on one side) wearing the clothing of conventions (which way is "east," what we call "sun"). The clothing is agreed; the body underneath is the world being consistent.

## Foundational ingredients (working list)

1. **A patterned world** — or there is nothing to learn.
2. **A learner** — a boundary, contact with the world, and a lasting trace (memory).
3. **The world-teaches engine** — expect, be wrong, adjust. Self-graded.
4. **A trusted source** — the only channel for conventions the world cannot teach.
5. **Trust**, which splits into *deciding who to trust* (built by ingredient 3) and *adopting from them without checking*.
6. **Relevance** — a reason to attend to this and not that. May not be primitive; might fall out of needs plus trust. Open.

## Open questions

- What exactly makes a source trustworthy to a fresh learner — only "consistent + meets my needs," or more?
- Is **relevance** (#6) its own foundation, or does it come from the others?
- Is "expecting what comes next" really the deepest root of learning, or is there something underneath it?
- Is "something that repeats" the simplest meaningful pattern to start a learner on, or is there a more basic one?

## What we are NOT yet deciding

The concrete, testable form of this experiment — what we would actually simulate or build — is deliberately still open. We develop it slowly, and only turn it into idea files (then code) once we are confident the foundation above is right.
